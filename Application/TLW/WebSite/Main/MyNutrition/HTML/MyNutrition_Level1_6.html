Decoding Label Terms

Food labels can be misleading. They can be so misleading, that in 1993 the U.S. Department of Agriculture imposed a food label standard to help with the confusion on product labels that sounded healthy, but really weren’t.

Words like “light”, “free”, and “reduced” were targets of the 1993 initiative. The idea was to ensure the labels were not tricking the buyer into thinking they were purchasing a healthy product. The new standards ensured that a salad dressing described as “light,” meant light in calories — not light in color. This certainly helped, but there are many misleading words that continue to appear on foods.

Play it safe: No matter what the description on the front of the box, read the numeric label on the side to determine the exact amounts of fat, calories, vitamins and minerals. Make sure to check the serving size, and hang on to your common sense. Even “light” cookies may not be the best choice when you’re trying to manage your weight.

 

What do the labels mean?

Some of the terms used to describe foods refer to a general quality of the food. Examples of this are “Lean” and “Light”. Other terms indicate that a specific nutritional component is lower or higher than usually expected for that particular product. For instance, a “high fiber” cereal may only be high in fiber as compared to another cereal — not based on recommended standards. The same goes for the term “low sodium”.
