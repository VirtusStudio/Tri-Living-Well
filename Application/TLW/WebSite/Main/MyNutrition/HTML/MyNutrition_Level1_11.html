Consuming Carbohydrates

Carbohydrates are getting a bad rap in the diet world, these days. In reality weight gain is caused by too many calories, not too many carbs.

Carbohydrates are an essential nutrient and the body’s main energy source. In fact, between 55% and 65% of your total calories should come from carbohydrates. Before you go carb crazy, it’s important to note there are different kinds of carbs and some are better for weight management than others. This article will help you understand how carbs work and how to make them work for you.

Doing carbs smart

Learn the GI. Our bodies are able to use carbs for energy because, after digestion, the liver breaks them down into simple sugars called Glucose. The Glycemic Index (GI) is a method of classifying carbohydrates based on how they affect our blood sugar levels. Foods with high GI values are not the best. These foods break down into sugars more quickly than foods with lower GI values. Originally designed for diabetes treatment, the GI can be used by everyone to determine healthy carbohydrate choices. The web site http://www.glycemicindex.com provides a searchable GI database so you can look up foods before you eat them.

Watch refined sugar. Simple, refined carbohydrates (like cookies or sugar cereals) have a high GI rating. You want to eat fewer of these because too many can cause your blood sugar and insulin levels to rise over time, which can lead to heart disease, hypertension, diabetes, and insulin resistance. 

Welcome whole grains: Lower GI foods are metabolized slowly, releasing glucose gradually into the blood stream. These tend to be complex carbohydrates — unrefined and rich in fiber, vitamins, and minerals. Whole grains should make up at least half of your total carbohydrate intake, because they can reduce the risk of stroke, Type 2 diabetes, and heart disease. Some good choices are whole wheat breads and pastas, brown rice, or oatmeal.


Enjoy Fruits: Fruits and vegetables are also considered to be carbohydrates. They are generally low in calories and filled with vitamins, minerals, and fiber. You can try them cooked, canned, or frozen for variety, but to get the most nutrition for the nibble, eat them raw.

The sample meal plans in this stage include the recommended amount of carbohydrates per day and whole grain sources of carbohydrates.

1600 to 1800 Calorie Sample Meals

# 1

 

Breakfast

1 packet cream of wheat

3 tsp. brown sugar

8 oz. 1% milk

 

Morning Snack

1 low-fat cheese string

1 orange

 

Lunch

1/2 Tuna Salad Sandwich and Tomato Soup

            1 slice whole wheat bread

            2 oz. tuna salad (packed in water)

            1 tbsp. light mayonnaise

            ½ c. low-salt tomato soup

1 garden salad (with tomatoes and cucumbers)

2 tbsp. low-fat dressing (of choice)

 

 Afternoon Snack

2 tbsp. hummus

1 small pita (4” dia.) cut into pieces

 

Dinner

Spaghetti with Shrimp

            1 c. spaghetti

            ½   c. spaghetti sauce (homemade or jar)

            3 oz. cooked shrimp

1 c. green beans

1 slice Italian bread

 

Evening Snack

2 strawberry fig newtons

 

TOTAL: 1643 calories
