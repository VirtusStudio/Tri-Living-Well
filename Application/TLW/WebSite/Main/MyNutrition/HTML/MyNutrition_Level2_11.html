Carb Craziness

 

Lately, everyone seems to be quitting sugar, eating burgers without buns, and cooking eggs instead of oatmeal for breakfast. While there’s nothing wrong with replacing processed carbs and simple sugars with whole grains and fruit, there’s no reason to go crazy. Lots of the current anti-carb rhetoric comes from a misunderstanding of what they are and how they function in the body.

 

 

 

 

What are Carbohydrates?

 

Carbohydrates are the basic building blocks for energy. They provide glucose, which fuels the brain and central nervous system. They contain lots of vitamins and minerals and are generally low in fat and calories (especially veggies and fruit) and are often quite delicious. There are two distinct types of carbs: simple and complex.

 

 

 

 

Complex Carbs

 

 

Complex carbohydrates include veggies and whole grains like whole wheat pasta, rice, oatmeal. Typically, they are high in fiber and take longer to digest. They also give the body a sense of fullness and satisfaction which goes a long way to allay less nutritious food cravings.

 Simple Carbs

 

 

Theses are the ones we generally eat too much of—sugar based cookies, candies, and syrup. These provide little more than calories and carbs and are not as nutritious. Still, they aren’t as wicked as they are often made out to be. Let’s take a look at some common carb myths.

 

 

 

Myth

Truth

 

Carbs make you fat

Eating too many calories- from any food source- is what makes you gain weight.

 

Combining carbs and protein make you gain weight faster

So false. Combining carbs and protein might actually help you lose weight, by stabilizing your blood sugar and giving you a consistent flow of energy, deterring binge eating.

 

Carbs lead to hyperactivity

There’s no medical evidence linking hyperactivity or Attention Deficit Disorder (ADHD) to high carb or sugar intake.

 

Sugar is addicting

Addiction is a physical and emotional dependency on a substance. Neither sugar nor any other carbohydrate is addicting. Liking a daily dose of chocolate doesn’t make you an addict; it makes you human.

 

Sugar free= calorie free

Only if there are no other sources of calories in the food, but often calories come from other carbs, fat, or protein. Read the nutrition label to find the facts.

Sample Recipe

 

Low-Fat French Toast

 

 

Serves: 4

Serving Size: 2 slices

 

Ingredients:

 

1 large egg plus 1 large egg white

¾ C canned fat-free evaporated milk

1 tsp vanilla extract

8 slices Pepperidge Farm Raisin Cinnamon Swirl bread

1 tsp confectioners’ sugar

 

Directions:

 

1. In a wide dish, beat the egg and egg white with a fork until smooth. Stir in milk and vanilla.

 

2. Set a griddle or large skillet over medium-high heat until hot (about 2 minutes). Remove pan from heat and coat with cooking spray; turn heat to medium and return pan to burner.

 

3. Dip the bread into the egg mixture to saturate. Lift out, letting excess drip off before placing on griddle. Cook until browned and cooked through (1 to 2 minutes per side). Transfer to plates. Dust with sifted confectioner's sugar.
