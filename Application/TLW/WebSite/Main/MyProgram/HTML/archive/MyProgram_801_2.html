Strength Training Tri Level Stage 2

Initially, we will keep things simple. We will teach 11 exercises, along with 2 optional movements. The workouts will not vary for the first four weeks as you strive to develop consistency. The first month is your foundation phase. In the future weeks, we will introduce variations and add changes to your program. So let’s get started and have some fun!

Weights, Repetitions and Pace

If you are new to Weight training or have not worked out for some time, you may have to experiment with selecting the proper weight for each exercise. Always remember, it is more important to perform an exercise properly and safely than struggle with too much weight. If you train consistently and safely, you will become stronger. If you focus on proper form and safety, the risk of injury is less likely. Gains in fitness are made through consistency and progression.

In this routine, perform 2 sets of each exercise. The exercises should be performed on machines. Later on, we will incorporate free weights and dumbbells. Perform 10-15 repetitions for each set. In the first set, strive to complete 15 repetitions. In the second set, aim for 15 reps, but perform a minimum of 10 repetitions. Don’t worry if you are experiencing difficulty. Progress will come with each workout.

In the initial workout, be conservative with weight selection, especially on the first set of each exercise. You should be able to tell within a few repetitions whether you can complete 15 reps. If the weight is too heavy, take some weight off and concentrate on form. If the weight is too light, complete the set, concentrating on form. In the next set, add more weight. Within a few workouts, you should have a clearer understanding of this process.

When you are able to perform 15 repetitions for both sets, it is time to increase the weight setting. Again, be sure to perform both sets using proper form before adding additional weight. Form first, weight second.

Each set should take approximately 1 minute to perform. Take a 1 minute recovery between sets and exercises. The entire routine should not extend beyond 45 minutes. However, if you are able, take less time between sets and increase the cardiovascular benefit by keeping your heart rate level higher throughout your workout.


Expectations

If you are beginning an exercise program for the first time or after an extended period of time, you may experience some soreness after a workout. This should taper over time. Soreness goes away in a couple of days, but pain lingers. It’s important to know the difference. Please don’t ever train through pain - see your doctor.

Muscles should feel tired after a workout. Initially, your workouts should not be intense as you are learning new exercises.  As you progress and intensity increases, expect to feel tired at the end of your workouts. Always remember to eat well, and get proper rest. If you follow this formula, expect to get stronger.

Once workouts become more challenging, you may find yourself breathing hard, as if running or climbing stairs. This is to be expected. A strength training workout can be “aerobic”, since muscles are requiring oxygen, similar to a cardio workout. Don’t shy away from this - go for it!

If you remain consistent, eventually you will build muscle. Consider this to be a positive occurrence. If you are concerned about weight gain, muscle will help you to burn more body fat. Keep this in mind if you get on the scale. Progress is not made overnight.

One more thing...have fun!

Hip Adduction (Outer Thigh) back to top

Sit in the machine so that your lower back is well supported, against the back pad. If the pad is adjustable, set it so that your “sit bones” line up over the axes of rotation of the movements arms. In other words, look under the seat, align the two rotation points in a vertical line with your “sit bones”. If you have a slender build, set the back pad as far back as it can go or in a  vertical position. If you have a large build, place the back pad in a horizontal position. Once your position is established, squeeze your legs together, causing the movement arms to meet. If you are unable to get the movement arms to meet, reduce the weight until you can achieve full range of motion.  After a short pause in the middle/contracted position, slowly lower the weight, moving your legs apart into the stretched position. Do this slowly to minimize potential soreness and/or injury.

Hip Adduction (Inner Thigh) back to top

Position yourself in the machine so that your back is well supported, against the back pad (including lower back). If you have a slender build, set the back pad completely or nearly vertical. If you have a large build, you’ll usually set the back pad down toward horizontal. Once your position is established, press the outside of your thighs against the pads on the movement arms, and press until you reach the maximum range of motion possible. Avoid arching your back to achieve additional range of motion. If your machine is designed with foot rests, avoid pressing your feet down against the footrests. Pause briefly at your maximum range of motion, then move slowly back inward. Do not allow the weight to come to rest. Repeat.

Leg Extention back to top

Position the back pad of the machine with your knee aligned with the axis of the machine’s rotation. If possible, adjust the shin pad to a position near the ankle, but not on top of the foot. (It may take some trial and error to find a comfortable spot). If your leg extension machine has a seat belt, adjust it so that it holds your hips down firmly.

There will likely be handles on each side of the seat; for now, grip them lightly. Slowly lift the weight by extending your legs, until you reach the highest point your range of motion will allow (to help you reach this point, grip the handles more firmly, and pull yourself down into the seat as you approach the top). Pause briefly in the fully extended position (approximately one second), and lower under control. Just before you reach bottom (the lifted weight sits down on top of the rest of the stack), repeat.

Note: the ideal top position will be one of full extension. There are a couple of reasons why you might not reach this position, however: 1 - you may have a knee issue, which you will have already discussed with your doctor. If your doctor advises you to stop short of full extension, please follow his/her advice. 2 - some machines are constructed in such a way that the top position feels incredibly heavy. In order to reach full extension on such a machine, you might need to reduce the weight so drastically as to make the exercise too easy. In such a case, you’ll need to select a weight that represents a “happy medium” and get the greatest range of motion possible on your machine.


Leg Curl
Position the machine so that your knee lines up with the axis of rotation of the machine, similar to the position described for the leg extension. The leg pad should be low on the leg, above the Achilles tendon, but not fully on the calf. Similar to the leg extension, your machine will likely have handles on either side of the seat; please use these to help keep your hips and low back stable. Flex your feet so that your toes point toward your knees. Begin by slowly bending your knees, pulling your hips back toward your butt. When you have reached the point of greatest contraction, pause briefly, and then slowly extend your legs again. Just before you set the weight stack down, repeat.
 


Leg Press
This will likely be one of (if not the most) the most demanding exercises in your workout, as it works a lot of muscle. Be conservative with weight and intensity increases on this one. If possible, set your seat so that, when you extend your legs, you stop just short of locking out your knees. This will help keep continuous tension on the leg muscles. If your leg press allows you to change the angle of the back pad, experiment. A more upright position tends to concentrate the work on the quadriceps (front of the thigh), while a more reclined position tends to get the hamstrings and gluteus muscles more involved, along with the quadriceps.  Once you are set, slowly and steadily extend your legs, until you reach the stopping point of the machine, or until you are just short of locking your knees out. There is no need for a pause in this position; simply turn the movement around, slowly and smoothly, until your legs are bent 90 degrees. Avoid bringing your legs back past this point, as your hips will usually elevate, and when it is time to press the weight out again, your lower back will be called upon to pull the hips back down. This is an unsafe position for the lower back to have to work in.
 


Machine Row
If the chest pad of your machine is adjustable, set it so that your arms can fully extend, but so that you only have a slight reach beyond this. This will enable you to easily set the weight down when your set is over. Most people start this movement with their arms, which means they get a great biceps (and to some degree, shoulder) workout, but not much back work. Try instead to initiate things by drawing your shoulders back and inward. This doesn’t always come naturally, so try this (without using any weight): imagine someone has their hand on your back. Try to squeeze the hand with your shoulder blades. Do this a few times, until you feel like you have some control over this movement, then do the same thing on the row machine. Grip the handles with your hands, draw your shoulders back and your shoulder blades inward. Once you have drawn shoulders and shoulder blades as far back and in as possible, finish the movement by bending your arms/drawing your elbows back behind you. Don’t lean back and away from the chest pad, as this transfers the work from the upper back/shoulders/arms to the lower back. Once you’ve reached the point where you can no longer draw your elbows back any farther, pause in this contracted position for a moment. Then, extend your arms again, slowly and under control, until straight. Repeat until your set is finished.
 


Chest Press
Position the seat so that the handles are even with your lower chest, at the nipple line. Move your elbows outward, so that they are roughly at the same level as your hands and forearms, and try to keep the elbows at that position throughout the movement. Press the handles away from your body until your arms are just short of the locked-out position, and then slowly and smoothly lower the weight, bending your arms until the handles are near your chest again/the weight is just short of coming to rest. Repeat, slowly and smoothly, until the set is finished.
 


Lat Pulldown
If your machine allows you to adjust your seat height, set this so that when you pull the handle(s) down toward your chest, they come to a pause at the mid/lower part of the chest. Grip the handles so that your palms face you. Similar to the machine row, try to start the movement by moving the shoulders; this time, you’ll move the shoulders and shoulder blades down, rather than back and inward. Once the shoulders have moved as  much as possible, complete the movement by bending the arms and drawing the elbows behind you. Pause for a moment in the bottom/contracted position, then extend the arms again, slowly and steadily. Repeat until the set is finished.
 


Overhead/Shoulder Press
Position yourself so that your back is fully supported, firmly against the back pad, including your lower back. If the seat height on your machine is adjustable, set this so that the handles are at or slightly below shoulder height (Note: if you have, or have had, shoulder issues, it may be better to lower the seat a bit, and set the handles slightly above shoulder height. Please check with your doctor). If a choice of hand positions is offered, use the handles that allow your elbows to be closer to your torso. Once you are set, simply press the weight upward, stopping just short of locking out your elbows. Slowly and smoothly lower the weight. Just before you reach the absolute bottom position, press upward again. Repeat until your set is finished.
 


Abdominal Machine
If possible, position the seat so that your shoulders are level with, or slightly below your elbows (this will vary, depending on machine design). Try to keep your shoulders down throughout the movement. Slowly and steadily, contract your abs, moving into a “crunch” position. Focus on curling the torso, rather than “bowing” forward. When you reach the point at which you can no longer curl the torso (point of greatest contraction), slowly and smoothly move in the other direction. Repeat until your set is finished. Note: it is very common to do this movement very quickly. For best results, and safety, please do this movement slowly, in both directions.
 


Hip Flexion/Leg Raise
This can be performed on a machine, sitting on the end of a bench, or on a leg raise stand/station. For beginners, use either a machine (not so common these days) or sit on the end of a bench. Place your hands on the bench, just behind you, for support. Then, simply lift your knees toward your chest, as far as you can go. Then, gradually straighten your legs and repeat.

On a leg raise station, step up on the steps of the machine, and place your back against the back pad. Place your forearms on the pads to either side of you, and lightly grip the handles. Take your feet off the steps, supporting your weight with your arms and shoulders. Raise your knees up toward your chest, as high as you can, and lower until straight. If you can, keep your legs slightly in front of you, in order to keep tension on the hip flexors at all times. Note: it is very common to do this movement very quickly. For best results, and safety, please do this movement slowly, in both directions.
