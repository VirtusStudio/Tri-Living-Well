Dietary Supplements – Are They Safe?

In 1997, the U.S. government banned some dietary supplements containing an herb called Ephedra. That’s because the young adults who took them were dying of sudden cardiac arrest. Tragic events like these have ignited a debate on the safety of dietary supplements, which are not regulated by the federal government. Still, many people rely on dietary supplements to help them lose weight or to enhance their athletic performance.

A 1994 federal law by The Food and Drug Administration’s (FDA) requires manufacturers to ensure safety before supplements are put on the market. But because the industry is unregulated, there are no guarantees that any dietary supplement works as intended, is safe, or even contains the ingredients listed on its label. Extreme caution is warranted here. Research carefully and consult with a knowledgeable health professional before taking any supplement.

 

Using supplements safely

What exactly are supplements? The FDA defines a dietary supplement as a product orally ingested that contains ingredients meant to compliment the food a person eats. That’s a pretty broad definition and part of the reason the industry is so hard to regulate. Ingredients in supplements can include vitamins, minerals, herbs, botanicals, amino acids, enzymes, metabolites, and organ tissues. Supplements are sold in various forms, such as softgels, powders, tablets, gelcaps, capsules, and liquids.

What are supplement side effects? The FDA is responsible for taking action when people report unsafe side effects from dietary supplements and have the power to ban products, as in the case of Ephedra. Other supplements have also been associated with serious adverse side effects, especially those containing androstenedione, yohimbine, and kava, which all increase testosterone levels.

Are there any safe supplements? There are many safe supplements that are part of our regular diets. Vitamins, caffeine, creatine, and protein powders can be taken safely in recommended doses. However, large or excess doses consumed over time can become harmful. That’s because the body doesn’t naturally produce vitamins or minerals, which are considered micronutrients and must come from food or dietary supplements. Even though we need these things, it’s better to get as much of them as possible from healthy eating.

What should supplements do? Supplements are not meant as substitutes for food, but rather to compliment your diet. Unlike food, supplements do not provide the nutrients, fiber, and phytochemicals that can help protect against cancer, diabetes, heart disease, and high blood pressure. Before adding any supplement to your diet, it’s important to talk to your doctor.
