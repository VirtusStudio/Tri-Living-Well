Are You Drinking Enough Water?

 

Next to the air we breathe, water is a vital component of physical health. Water comprises 60 to 70 percent of a normal adult’s weight. An individual can last almost two months without food, but only a matter of days without water.

 

Water performs a number of physiological functions in the body: it carries nutrients and oxygen through the blood, helps to regulate body temperature through perspiration, it lubricates joints, and it keeps skin supple. It also facilitates the transfer of waste by the kidneys. Most people take drinking water as an afterthought, but it’s essential to your health. Here are some key facts about water and some tips to make sure you’re getting enough H2O.

 

Answers for Wetting Your Whistle

 

How do I know if I’m dehydrated?
Thirst is not always an accurate barometer of the need for water. Initial symptoms of dehydration include headaches, weakness and loss of concentration. With excessive heat and perspiration, you may need water before you actually feel it. This is because dehydration affects the high-water-content parts of the body first, such as blood (90% water), brain (85% water) and muscles (70% water). Continuing in a dehydrated state will eventually cause the body to begin to shut down. Chronic dehydration can contribute to a number of health problems such as constipation and kidney stones.

 

What happens if I don’t drink enough water?
Insufficient intake of water can impact every aspect of physiology, including weight management. If you’re not drinking enough water, your body might retain excess body fat and water in an effort to hang on to the water you already have. When the kidneys do not get enough fluid, the liver stops metabolizing fat adequately. Drinking more water cuts down on fluid retention, which also aids in weight loss. In other words, by drinking water, you actually lose water weight.

 

How much water should I drink?
Eight cups of water (64 fluid ounces or 2 liters) is the MINIMUM amount of fluid recommended daily.  People who are overweight should drink an additional 8 ounces for every 25 pounds they exceed their ideal weight. To maintain optimal hydration, consuming 2.5 to 3 liters (10.4 – 12.5 cups) of water daily is recommended.

 

How can I tell if I’m getting enough water?
A good way to determine if you’re drinking enough is to look at the color of your urine. If it is clear or pale, then your fluid intake is probably fine. If your urine is dark yellow, you probably need to drink more. So, the next time you are at a restaurant, instead of having a soda or fruit beverage, why not stick with a tall, cool glass of water. It may seem boring, but your body will certainly appreciate it.

 

When should I drink water?
Your intake of water should be spread throughout the day and evening. Over a period of time, your bladder tends to adjust to an increase in fluids, and you will urinate less frequently but in larger amounts.

 

Do other food and drinks contain water, and does that count?
Ideally, you should drink at least 64 ounces of pure water, but you can also add in other beverages and some foods. The best substitutes for plain H2O are beverages that are greater than 90% water by volume. (See the table below.)

 

Beverages such as tea, coffee and fruit juices count towards fluid intake, but can contain sugar, alcohol, caffeine and artificial flavors. Sugar and alcohol add calories. Caffeine acts as a diuretic and alcohol is a dehydrating agent, so both of these sap water from your system. Many artificial flavorings are treated as toxins by the body. Try to use these just for variety, but make sure you get most of your fluid from good old H2O.

 

Tips for Getting More Water

 

An easy way to increase the amount of water in your diet is to incorporate drinking water into your daily routine. Once you begin to consume 8 to 10 glasses of water a day, you could be on your way to a healthier and leaner body.

 

·         When you wake in the morning, start with a glass of water.

·         Find time in the day to sit down with a regular drink – water, tea, and juice.

·         Keep a bottle of water with you at all times – pocketbook, briefcase, and gym bag.

·         Make it a habit to have a glass of water with every meal.

·         When exercising, make it a rule to regularly drink water every 10 -15 minutes. The sensation of thirst is not triggered until you are already dehydrated.

·         Increase the number of servings of fresh fruits and vegetables as they have high water content.
