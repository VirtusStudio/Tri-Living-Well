Laxative and Diuretic Abuse

 

It would seem that the more waste you can eliminate from your body, the less you should weigh. But forcing yourself to go to the bathroom is a dangerous and counterproductive way to lose weight. Laxative and diuretic abuse can not only lead to weight gain, it can also lead to some serious health risks.

 

 

 

 

 

The Realities of Laxative and Diuretic Abuse

 

Laxatives

How they work: They force bowel movements by stimulating the large intestine. They are the most commonly abused substance by patients with eating disorders.

 

Effectiveness: Laxatives are an ineffective way to lose weight because they help you empty your bowels after the calories you’ve eaten have been absorbed. Any weight loss will therefore come from losing water weight, and will quickly come back after you stop taking the pills.

Risks: Using laxatives for anything other than occasional constipation is extremely dangerous. It is also ineffective as a weight control tool. Because the body becomes dehydrated during abuse, it compensates by retaining fluid. When abusers stop using laxatives, they usually gain from 5-15 pounds in water weight. It takes weeks after the abuse has ended for the hormone levels to return to normal, allowing the kidneys to release excess fluid and weight.

 

 

 

Diuretics

How they work: Diuretics force urination, which eliminates water from the body.

Effectiveness:  Not effective at all. The only weight lost is water weight.

Risks:  Diuretics can be used effectively to treat diseases and conditions, but not for weight control. The risks with diuretic abuse result from dehydration and electrolyte imbalance.

 

Risks include:

 
  • Irregular heartbeat
  • Cardiac arrhythmia
  • Renal failure
  • Heart attack
  • Death

 

 

Too Good to Be True

 

Perhaps people turn to laxatives and diuretics because it doesn’t involve any work on the part of the dieter. This quick fix mentality is ineffective and often dangerous.  Any use of laxatives or diuretics other than their intended medical use is abuse. There is no effective way to lose weight and keep it off other than slowly, by moderately reducing your calorie intake and exercising.

Sample Recipe

 

Chicken Pesto Pinwheel Snack

 

Serves: 10

 

Ingredients:

 

1 cup packed baby spinach leaves

1 large boneless, skinless chicken breast (10 ounces)

2 teaspoons basil pesto

Ground black pepper

 

 

Directions:

 

1. Preheat the oven to 375°F. Tear off a 12" x 12" piece of heavy-duty aluminum foil and set aside. Place the spinach in a nonstick skillet. Cover and cook over medium-high heat, tossing occasionally, for about 2 minutes, or until wilted. Drain off any excess water. Pat dry with paper towels. Set aside.

 

2. Lay the chicken breast on a work surface. If the fillet is attached, open it away from the breast like opening a book. With a sharp knife, cut lengthwise through the thickest part of the breast almost but not all the way through. Open that flap like the page of a book. With a heavy skillet or scaloppini pounder, flatten the chicken to 1/2" thickness. Spread the pesto evenly over the top side of the chicken. Scatter the reserved spinach over the pesto.

 

3. Starting with a long side of the chicken, roll into a tube. Transfer the tube to the piece of foil. Roll tightly to secure the tube. Twist the foil ends to secure. Place the tube, seam side up, on a small baking sheet.

 

4. Bake for about 50 minutes, or until an instant-read thermometer inserted in the center of the tube registers 170°F. Remove and let stand for 10 minutes.

 

5. Carefully unwrap the foil. Cut into 20 thin slices and season to taste with pepper. Serve warm.
