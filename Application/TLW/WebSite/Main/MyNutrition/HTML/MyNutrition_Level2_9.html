Diet Aids:  Behind the Hype

 

There are lots of safe ways to get help losing and maintaining weight, but popping pills is not one of them.  The ads for over the counter or even herbal weight-loss supplements claim they will give you energy, burn fat, and optimize exercise. But in fact they can strip your body of nutrients and screw up your metabolism. Worse, many have been associated with heart attacks and strokes. And don’t let the word natural fool you—just because a product is natural doesn’t mean it’s necessarily good for you.

 

Be Warned

 

The Federal Drug Administration (FDA) doesn’t regulate diet aids before they hit the shelves, so no one knows if the labels are accurate, let alone if the products are effective. The Dietary Supplement Act of 1994 allows marketing of these products without mandatory testing for safety or effectiveness. Once on the market, the FDA has the burden of proving a given supplement is unsafe before banning it and limited authority in enforcing restrictions and bans.  

 

And the diet industry is great at finding loop holes—when one active ingredient is pulled out in compliance with a ban or recommendation, the next crop of products evolves, always seemingly one step ahead of the law. In other words, just because it’s on the shelf or a click away as you surf the web, does not mean it is safe or even legal.

Here’s a closer look at some specific diet aids, their claims, and their risks:

 

 

Banned in the USA

 

The following ingredients once commonly found in over the counter dietary aids have since been banned by the FDA. 

 

Herbal Hazard: Ephedra

How it works: A stimulant, ephedra constricts the blood vessels, increasing blood pressure and heart rate. It is addictive and works like amphetamines (or “speed”) to rev up the metabolism.

Common products that have contained Ephedra:
  • Xenadrine
  • Thermaline
  • Metabolife
  • ThermaPro
  • Metab-Rx Ultra
  • ThermoSlim
  • Extreme Ripped Force

Effectiveness: While ephedra may provide short term weight loss it is by no means worth the risk. Dietary supplements that contain ephedra have been banned in the United States since April 2004. As a result, many of these “aids” have been reformulated with equally questionable and unproven ingredients.

Risks: The FDA has linked numerous deaths and adverse effects directly to ephedra.

 

 

Prescription for Danger: PPA (Phenylpropanolamine)

How it works: PPA is an appetite suppressant that was a key ingredient in some widely recognizable dietary aids such as Dexatrim and Acutrim. It was also commonly used in cold remedies. In 2000 the FDA asked all over the counter products to remove PPA in response to a Yale University study that found an elevated risk for stroke.

Effectiveness: PPA is not thought to be effective for long term weight loss or maintenance.  

Risks: The biggest risk with PPA is hemorrhagic stroke, the kind of stroke that’s caused by bleeding in the brain. Women are especially at risk. 

 

 

Fact or Fiction?

 

These ingredients are still commonly available yet their effectiveness is far from proven.

 

 

Carnitine

How it works

 

Carnitine, a vitamin-like substance, transports fatty acids into the muscle where they can be burned for energy. It is often promoted as a “fat burner.”

Effectiveness

 

 None—we get all the carnitine we need from a combination of animal products in our diet and our bodies own natural synthesizing process.

 

 

Risks

 

The biggest risk here is to your wallet.

Chitosan

 

Called the Fat Trapper or Fat Burner

How it works

 

Chitosan is a fiber-like material made from the shells of lobsters, crabs, and shrimp. It binds fat in the small intestine. Called the “fat trapper,” advertisers claim this prevents your body from absorbing the fat.

Effectiveness

 

Minimal. While Chitosan does bind small amounts of fat, it’s hardly enough to effectively prevent you from gaining weight.

Risks

 

No specific health risks have been reported. However, there is the more personal risk of binging on fatty foods under the false belief that those calories won’t count.

 

 

Chromium Picolinate

 

How it works

 

A researcher for the USDA created this product by combining chromium with picolinic acid. It is marketed as a weight loss tool that helps the body process carbohydrates and fats. The claim is also made that this supplement can increase muscle and reduce body fat.

 

 

Effectiveness

 

Inconclusive but unlikely unless you are not getting enough chromium from your regular diet. Most of us are as we only need the trace amounts found in such common foods as beef, chicken, whole grain, apples and bananas.

Risks

 

A Dartmouth University study found possible links to cancer. A University of Alabama study showed that it reacts with vitamin C to produce a compound that could cause genetic mutations.

Sample Recipe

 

Roast Beef Wraps with Horseradish-Mustard

 

 

Serves: 4

 

Ingredients:

 

For the horseradish-mustard sauce
1/4 cup freshly grated horseradish
1 teaspoon Dijon mustard
1/2 cup non-fat sour cream
1/2 teaspoon sugar
salt to taste

For the wraps


4 large flour tortillas
3/4 pound sliced, lean roast beef tenderloin, from the deli
4 lettuce leaves, green leaf, bibb or romaine, shredded
4 large slices of ripe tomato, sliced very thin

 

Directions:

 

For the horseradish-mustard sauce:


1. In a small mixing bowl, combine the horseradish, mustard, sour cream, sugar and salt.

For the wraps:

1. Lay the tortillas out and spread 1 tablespoon of the sour cream mixture on each of them. Divide the roast beef among the tortillas and spread the remaining sour cream mixture on top of the beef. Divide the shredded lettuce among the tortillas and top with a tomato slice. Tightly roll each tortilla into a cylinder, ending with the seam side down.
(The wraps can be stored in the refrigerator for up to 3 days.)

2. Cut the wraps in half on the diagonal and serve.

 

 

 

Exchanges:  1 serving = 3 protein, 2 starch, 1 fat
