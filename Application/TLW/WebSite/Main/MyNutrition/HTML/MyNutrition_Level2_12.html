Calcium is Cool

 

It’s not just little kids and women concerned with osteoporosis that need calcium. Calcium is the most abundant mineral in the human body. It makes strong bones and teeth and performs a host of other functions in the body. Luckily, it’s easy to come by in a range of tasty foods, even if you don’t eat dairy. Here are some cool facts about calcium and some suggestions about how to incorporate more into your diet.

 

 

 

How it works

 

Building Bones: Bones and teeth are made from living tissue that continually break down and need to be rebuilt. The rate and formation of that rebuilding changes as we grow and age. After reaching peak density at age 30, breakdown levels begin to exceed rebuilding. So, the stronger your bones are by your third decade, the more likely you are to delay bone loss and avoid osteoporosis. This is especially true for post-menopausal women.

 

You need to eat enough calcium throughout your life to prevent bone loss and osteoporosis. About 1,000-1,500 milligrams per day will do for most adults, though recommendations vary slightly by age and sex.

Getting Enough Calcium

 

Dairy does it: The USDA’s Food Guide Pyramid recommends 2-3 servings of dairy products per day. This is because dairy products provide the greatest source of calcium in the diets of most Americans. Non-fat and reduced fat dairy products generally contain the same amount of calcium as regular products. Here are some single serving sized suggestions:

 

  • 8 ounces of yogurt

 

  • 1 cup or 8 ounces of milk

 

  • 1.5 ounces of natural cheese

 

  • 2 ounces of processed cheese, such as American

 

Non-dairy alternatives: Lots of people avoid dairy for a range of reasons. Some are lactose intolerant. Others maintain vegan diets. In some cultures dairy is practically nonexistent. So it’s a good thing there are plenty of alternatives for packing in calcium. Here are some yummy examples:

 
  • Kale
  • Broccoli
  • Spinach
  • Navy beans
  • Almonds
  • Tofu
  • Chinese cabbage
  • Fortified cereals
  • Fortified drinks

 

Calcium inhibitors: Too much sodium, protein, or caffeine can rob your body of calcium. Try not to consume more than 10-15% of your calories from sodium in a given day. Caffeine actually prevents calcium from being absorbed, so consume it in moderation. But if you have to have your morning cup of joe, try adding a tablespoon of milk to offset the loss. Alcohol and soda (which contains phosphates) may also contribute to calcium loss, so enjoy these sparingly.

Sample Recipe

 

Baked Penne with Zucchini

 

 

Serves: 6

 

Ingredients:

 

2 c. whole wheat pasta

2 tsp extra virgin olive oil

2 med zucchini, cut into small cubes

 ¾ lb green beans, trimmed and cut diagonally in half

1 can no salt added diced tomatoes

1 C tomato-basil pasta sauce

½ c. water

½ tsp salt-free Italian seasoning

¼ tsp kosher salt

1/8 tsp freshly ground black pepper

½ C shredded reduced-fat mozzarella cheese

2 tbsp grated Parmigiano-Reggiano cheese

 

Directions:

 

1. Preheat oven to 375 °F. Prepare pasta per package directions (reduce cooking time by 2 to 3 minutes). Drain.

 

2. Heat oil in large nonstick skillet over medium-high heat. Add zucchini and green beans. Cover and cook, stirring occasionally, 8 minutes or until vegetables are lightly browned. Stir in tomatoes (with juice), pasta sauce, water, Italian seasoning, salt, and pepper. Cover and simmer 4 minutes.

 

3. Transfer half of the pasta into 2-quart baking dish coated with cooking spray. Top with half of the vegetable mixture. Add remaining pasta and vegetables. Sprinkle with cheeses. Cover and bake 30 minutes or until bubbly. Uncover and bake 10 minutes longer.
