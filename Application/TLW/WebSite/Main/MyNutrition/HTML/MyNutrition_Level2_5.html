Shopping for Wellness

 

Making healthy food choices begins in the grocery store. But supermarkets reel you in with flashy photos, targeted product placements, aisle layouts and weekly specials all strategically designed to make you buy more food. (Yes, everyone knows how easy it is to load up on ice cream when the store’s having a two for one special.)  

 

But even successfully navigating your way past all that temptation is no guarantee you’ll make the right choices as some packages even make the food inside seem healthier than it is. Shopping well means eating well, though, so the effort to shop for wellness is worth it.

 

 

 

    Tips for Healthy Shopping

 

 

 

Do

Don’t

Plan ahead

 

Create a weekly menu of healthy meals. Search for recipes that include nutritional information so you’ll know the calorie and nutritional content of the dish. Then, make a list of all the ingredients you’ll need for those dishes, plus healthy snacks, cereals, and odds and ends. Stick to your list and stay away from impulse buys.

 

Shop hungry

 

It’s so easy to be tempted by all the wrong stuff when you’re starving! You might not even wait until you get home before you break open a bag. So many grocery stores now offer hot food bars, cafes, and samples. Make sure to shop after a meal or a healthy snack to avoid buying impulsively or eating your way through the aisles.

 

 

Shop the parameter

 

Most grocery stores are laid out with a ring of fresh foods surrounding aisles of processed foods. Sticking to the parameter means you’ll encounter produce, meats, fish, and dairy. Know what you want from the aisles and get out before you begin to rationalize “needing” the giant box of cookies!

 

 

Go down every aisle

 

Why have a stare-down with a box of Oreos that you may or may not win? There’s just no reason to go down each and every aisle in the market—and every reason not to.

 

Read  labels

 

Reading food labels is the only way to find out the ingredients and nutritional content. You might be surprised how many crackers use cottonseed oil, for instance, or how many cereals sweeten with high fructose corn syrup.

 

 

Believe the hype

 

Don’t let healthy-looking packaging be the determining factor. Be wary of packages touting healthy buzzwords like “multigrain” or “low-carb.” Sure, butter’s low-carb, but it’s also pure fat.

Hit the freezers

 

Keep frozen fruits and vegetables in stock, toss them into stir-fries or steam up quickly. Even if you prefer to eat fresh, frozen veggies can be a lifesaver for the times you run out of the fresh stuff.

Be a “fresh only” snob

 

There’s just no point to it. The

FDA has confirmed that frozen fruits and vegetables are just as nutritious as fresh produce. And they have a way longer shelf-life.

Sample Recipe

 

Steamed Little Neck Clams with Tomatoes Over Angel Hair Pasta

 

Serves: 4

 

Ingredients:

 

2 pounds little neck clams, whole
1 tablespoon minced garlic
1 pound angel hair pasta
2 cups chopped tomatoes, canned or fresh
1/2 cup chopped Italian parsley
salt to taste
freshly ground black pepper

 

Directions:

 

1. Wash the clams. Place them, with just the water that clings to them, in a covered pot over high heat until they just open, about 4 to 6 minutes. Remove from the heat and set aside.

 

2. Meanwhile, bring a gallon of well-salted water to a boil.

 

3. Remove the open clams from the pot and reserve the juice.

 

4. Heat the reserved clam juice over medium-high heat and reduce the quantity by half.

 

5. Add the pasta to the boiling water.

 

6. Add the tomatoes to the clam juice and simmer. Season with salt and pepper. Add the parsley.

 

7. Cook the pasta until it is al dente, about 6 to 8 minutes.

 

8. In a large bowl, toss the pasta with the tomato mixture and arrange the clams. Serve immediately.
