Stage 7 – Dieting for Life – Living

 

 

Dieting for Life

 

Everyone’s heard that fad diets don’t work and might even be dangerous. So why do so many people try them? It’s hard to resist those glittery promises: Lose your belly in two weeks! Wear your wedding dress in just a few days!

 

Unfortunately, there really is no such thing as a quick fix. The only way to lose weight is to match your energy input –the amount of calories you eat – with your energy output – the amount of calories you burn. Burn more calories than you’re taking in, you’ll lose weight. Do it too quickly, and you might get burned. Here are some fad diets to watch out for and a sensible plan to help you lose and maintain your weight.

 

 

 

The Fad

Low-Carb Diets

Detox Diets

 

The Pitch

Eliminate or significantly restrict carbohydrates in your diet and shed excess fat.

 

Lose weight in a few short days by consuming specific foods or herbs that cleanse your body of toxins.

The Real Skinny

Without carbs, ketones (a waste product released as your body burns stored fat) collect in the body. Ketones have a diuretic effect, eliminating water (not fat) from your body. As soon as you start eating carbs again, you gain the water weight back.

 

The human body is naturally designed to cleanse itself so there is no need to engage in a detox diet to facilitate this process. Plus, once you tire of living on birdfeed, the weight comes right back on.

The Risks

Possible increased risk of heart disease as this diet replaces carbs with protein, saturated fat, and cholesterol.

Diabetics must be especially cautious, because a high protein diet can worsen diabetic kidney disease.

Potentially very dangerous; these diets can lead to dehydration, digestive problems and mineral imbalances. The low calorie intake forces the body into starvation mode— metabolizing lean muscle as energy and lowering your metabolic rate.

 

 

Diet Books: Separating Fact from Fiction

 

 

 

 

Does the diet drastically reduce calories?

 

The grapefruit diet, the Hollywood diet, the cabbage soup diet —sure they all work at first because they restrict calories to just a few hundred per day. That is not only ineffective —you’re sure to fall off the wagon or gain weight back as soon as you begin eating reasonable portions again—it’s also dangerous. Taking in too few calories means you’re missing out on important nutrients and slowing down metabolism. Unless you’re being guided to do so by your doctor, don’t follow any diet that asks you to eat under 1500–1800 calories per day.

 

Is it realistic?

 

Avoid restrictive plans with complicated rules. Anything that limits certain foods or a whole food group like carbs is suspect. Same goes for a diet that suggests you can lose weight simply by eating specific combinations of foods or timing your meals to precise intervals. Here are some common current diets that sound more like hocus pocus:

 
  • Eating based on your blood type
  • Eating only protein and fat
  • Drinking all of your calories
  • Eating only foods with no sugar

 

 

Does it require you to buy specific supplements or wonder-foods?

 

Forget about any program that insists on high-priced supplements or foods that claim to have magic fat-burning powers. Most won’t be effective; others may pose serious health risks.

 

 

Do you trust the source?

 

You don’t need a medical degree or even a dietician’s license to publish a diet book. Not all diets need to come from PhD’s but you should be skeptical about where the author got his or her information. Ask yourself if the recommendations seem to be based on real medical research. Some reliable sources for such research are the Journal of the American Medical Association (JAMA) and the Journal of the American Dietetic Association. If someone is quoting studies to promote a product, ask to see the results of those studies over time and check out when they were published.

 

 

Point System Diets

 

These can be healthy choices. They offer a simplified method of selecting foods based on calculated nutritional content. The system generally considers weight goals and how many calories you’ll need each day to lose weight. They can be a healthy way to monitor your energy intake and output if they promote a healthy distribution of carbohydrates, fats, and proteins. They should also incorporate exercise and limit weight loss to 1 to 2 pounds per week.

 

 

The No-Trick Solution

 

Day by day: A healthy diet does not have a beginning or an end. Diet modification is a lifestyle change. It must be practiced daily throughout your life for proper nutrition and weight management. That doesn’t mean never eating anything tasty or living on rabbit food. It does mean developing a healthy eating consciousness that guides you to make better choices most of the time. And it does mean enjoying a healthy diet that includes a balance of carbohydrate, fat, and protein. You’ll lose the weight gradually and keep it off. It might be slow, but, it’ll be a lot more satisfying!

Sample Recipe

 

Swordfish with Sun-dried Tomatoes and Capers

 

Serves: 4

Serving size: 1 steak

 

Ingredients:

 

1 tablespoon olive oil
2 cloves garlic, minced
8 sun-dried tomatoes, rehydrated and chopped
1 tablespoon capers
salt to taste
freshly ground black pepper
1/2 cup balsamic vinegar
4 swordfish steaks, about 4 to 6 ounces each

              

Directions:

 

1. Preheat the grill or broiler.

 

2. Heat the olive oil in a small skillet over medium heat. Add the garlic and cook for 2 minutes. Add the tomatoes and capers, season with salt and pepper and cook for 2 more minutes. Add the balsamic and cook until it has reduced to a syrup, about 3 minutes, and set aside.

 

3. Meanwhile, season the swordfish steaks with salt and pepper. Grill or broil the fish on each side until it is cooked through, about 6 minutes per side.

 

4. Place each steak on a plate and spoon the tomato mixture over it.
