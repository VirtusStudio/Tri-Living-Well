Accepting Your Weight

Dieting can be a vicious cycle. You lose weight, but you gain it right back. That’s because your body is actually programmed to maintain a certain weight, what health professionals call a “set point.”

As you learn about set point theory, you’ll understand the importance of learning to accept your natural weight. Rather than trying so hard to change our bodies, we could take a different approach; trying to change society’s judgmental attitude.

A different approach to weight management:

Here are the basics of set point theory. Every one of our bodies is programmed to weigh within a certain range called a “set point”. Your body will actually fight to maintain that weight. Your set point is genetically determined, like your height and your hair and eye color. And just like other biological characteristics, set points are unique to each person. That’s why it's not a good idea to go by the weight charts that you see in medical books or hanging in your doctor's office.

For instance, a woman at a height of 5'5" with a small frame may have a set point range between 120-130 lbs, but another woman with the same frame may have a set point range between 130-140 lbs. Neither is right or wrong, just like blue eyes are not better than brown ones.

What happens when you go out of your set point? When you go below your body's natural set point, your metabolism slows down to try to conserve energy. Your body senses it's in a state of semi-starvation and tries to use the few incoming calories most effectively. You may start to sleep more and your body temperature will drop. You might experience an uncontrollable urge to binge, a signal that your body needs more food. In extreme cases, women will stop having menstrual periods. The body’s reproductive system shuts down because it no longer has enough energy to handle a pregnancy.

Just as your metabolism will slow down when you go below your body's set point, it will increase if you go above it. Your body will try to fight against the weight gain by increasing the metabolism and raising the body's temperature in an effort to burn off the unwanted calories.

Using with set point theory to find a healthy weight: Most people who have tried dieting know how hard it is to lose weight and keep it off. It’s common to shed a few pounds in the first few weeks of dieting, but then gain them right back — despite a restricted food intake. That’s a sign that their bodies are trying to retain their set points. After a few weeks of this tug-of-war with the scale, they become frustrated and give up.

There is no test available to determine your body's natural set point. The best plan is to eat healthy foods and exercise moderately. Stay away from extreme dieting, which can actually do long term damage. After dieting, it could take up to a year of normal eating for your body to regain proper metabolic to function. Then you will find yourself drifting easily into your proper weight range.
