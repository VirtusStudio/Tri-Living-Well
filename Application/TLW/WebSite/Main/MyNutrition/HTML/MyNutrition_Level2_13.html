Using Caffeine Safely

 

Caffeine is a drug that provides energy.  It’s in everything from coffee and tea to chocolate and soda. It’s even found in pain relievers. For most people, consuming just a little of these won’t cause any problems. But, as with any drug, too much dependency can lead to addiction. If you are getting the proper amounts of sleep, water, and nutrition, you shouldn’t need caffeine to feel energized. That doesn’t mean you have to give up your morning wake-up cup—but you should evaluate your diet to make sure you’re getting a steady stream of fuel via nutrition throughout the day.

 

Buzz Worthy Facts 

 

 

How it works: By stimulating the central nervous system, caffeine causes a brief energy boost, mood elevation, and increases in alertness. It does not build up in the body and is usually excreted within several hour of consumption.

 

An acceptable level of consumption:

 

 

Caffeine (milligrams per day)

Adults

200-300 milligrams/day

 

Children and Teens

As little as possible, less than 100 milligrams*

 

* Studies show that consuming only 100 milligrams per day can cause dependency. That’s not very much, when you consider that a standard cup of coffee has about 115 milligrams of caffeine.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Risks: Caffeine affects people differently. Some people find that drinking it late in the day affects their sleep, while others don’t notice any symptoms. However, for all people, caffeine acts as a diuretic and a laxative. Because it makes you go to the bathroom, overuse can lead to dehydration, so be sparing in hot weather and during exercise.

 

Another possible side effect is addiction. Most caffeine-addicted people aren’t even aware they are hooked on the stuff- unless and until they try to quit. Withdrawal symptoms can range from mild to severe, but you can create an easier transition by reducing your caffeine consumption gradually.

Sample Recipe

 

Lime Chicken with Lentils and Dried Fruit

 

 

Serves: 4

 

Ingredients:

 

4 bone in chicken thighs (about 1 1/4lb)

1/4 tsp salt

1/8 tsp ground allspice

1/8 tsp ground red pepper

1 tbsp olive oil

3/4  C sm green and brown lentils, and a few red lentils, picked over and rinsed

3 scallions cut into 1” diagonal pieces

4 lg dried peaches (4 oz) cut into thin strips (if not available, substitute about 8 dried apricots)

1/2 C dried sour cherries or cranberries

2 tsp finely chopped crystallized ginger

1/3 C chopped cilantro

1½ C reduced sodium chicken broth

4 tsp finely grated lime zest

1 tbsp fresh lime juice

lime wedges (garnish)

 

Directions:

 

1. Preheat oven to 350ºF. Season chicken with half of the salt, allspice, and pepper. In ovenproof Dutch oven, heat oil over medium high heat. Cook chicken about 5 minutes on each side until browned. Transfer to plate.

 

2. Add lentils, scallions, peaches, cherries, ginger, half of the cilantro, and the remaining salt, allspice, and pepper. Cook, stirring, 2 minutes. Pour in broth. Return chicken to Dutch oven and submerge. Bring to a boil over high heat.

 

3. Remove from heat, cover, and bake 35 minutes. Uncover and bake 30 minutes longer or until lentils are cooked through. Remove chicken skins. Stir lime zest and juice into lentil mixture.

 

4. Arrange lentils on serving plates, top with chicken, and sprinkle with the remaining cilantro. Garnish with lime wedges.

 

 

 

 

 

Exchanges:  1 serving = 3 protein, 2 starch, 1 fat
