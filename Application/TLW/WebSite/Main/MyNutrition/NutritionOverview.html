Philosophy/Goals back to top

 

Nutrition and diet play an important role in achieving optimal health, fitness level, and weight.  Variety, moderation, and balance are the keys to healthy eating.  Eating nutritiously does not have to be about deprivation.  Deprivation does not work in the long run.  To achieve the long-term health and weight benefits of good nutrition, it is important to make a lifestyle change. 

Our goal is to offer you the opportunity to enhance your quality of life through health-promoting dietary and nutritional practices.  We will guide you in changing unhealthy eating behaviors and in developing a balanced diet for life.  Throughout this 6 month program, we will help you to develop the knowledge base and personal habits critical for optimal health and fitness level and for successful weight management.  The program will guide you in finding the appropriate amount of calories and nutrients for your body.

 

Getting Started back to top

We have provided a calorie calculator to help you determine your daily calorie needs.  This calculation is only an estimate.  If you are unable to achieve your weight goals by following the meal plans provided, that correspond to your calorie needs (based on the calculation), you may need to adjust your calorie intake/meal plan and/or exercise regimen or work personally with a Registered Dietitian for a more individualized plan.

Our goal is to familiarize you with portion sizes, the appropriate balance of nutrients, and examples of food choices to maximize your health and achieve your ideal body weight.  Below is a description of the food-balancing program called the exchange-meal plan.  We will guide you in using this method of meal planning to help you achieve your health and weight goals.  This type of meal plan uses serving sizes as the building blocks of your diet.

The word exchange refers to the fact that each item on a particular list may be interchanged with any other food item on the same list.  An exchange can be explained as a serving, substitution, or choice.  For this plan, foods are divided into six groups/lists:  starch, fruit, milk, fat vegetable, and meat.  The plan sets a serving size (amount) for each food, and, within each group/list, each serving or exchange has a similar amount of protein, carbohydrates, fat, and calories.

Each of the food groups/lists makes a specific contribution to the daily diet and it is important to remember that no ONE exchange category can supply all the nutrients needed for a well-balanced diet.  You need to eat foods from all six exchange lists every day to make sure that your diet is balanced and that you remain healthy. 

The advantage of the exchange-meal plan is that it provides a system in which a wide selection of foods can be included, thereby offering variety and versatility.  This plan also provides a framework to group foods with similar carbohydrates, protein, fat, and caloric content.  By selecting a variety of foods from each of the groups/lists, you are able to incorporate healthy food choices and balance into your diet.  Finally, the exchange-meal plan emphasizes important management concepts, such as carbohydrate amounts, fat modification, and calorie control, as well as, accountability.

Using the Exchange List back to top

Cereals, grains, pasta, breads, crackers, snacks, starchy vegetables, cooked beans, and peas are on starch list.  In general, one starch exchange is a 1/2 cup cereal, grain, or starchy vegetable; one slice of bread; 1/3 cup of rice or pasta, or 3/4 cup to an ounce of most snack foods.

Fresh, frozen, canned, and dried fruit and fruit juices are on the fruit list.  In general, one fruit exchange is one small to medium fresh fruit; 1/2 cup of canned or fresh fruit or fruit juice; or 1/4 cup of dried fruit.

Different types of milk and milk products, such as yogurt are on the milk list.  One cup (8 oz.) of fat-free milk or 2/3 cup (6 oz.) of fat-free or low-fat flavored yogurt sweetened with a non-nutritive sweetner are examples of one exchange.   Skim and reduced fat milks are recommended for adults and children over two years old, rather than whole milk.

The vegetable list includes all fresh, frozen, and dried vegetables.  Although three servings of vegetables are the equivalent of one starch serving, we count vegetables separately to encourage more consumption of them.
 
Meat and meat substitutes that contain both protein and fat are on the meat list.  In general, one exchange is one ounce of meat, fish, poultry, cheese, and 1/2 cup of beans.  Meat choices for the very lean or lean meat lists are encouraged.

 

One fat exchange is one teaspoon of regular margarine, mayo, or vegetable oil; one tablespoon of regular salad dressing or reduced-fat mayo; or two tablespoons of reduced fat salad dressings. When possible, monounsaturated or polyunsaturated fats should be substituted for saturated fats.

Some foods are in one list, but they may fit in another list.  For example, foods in the starch, fruit, and milk lists, each contribute similar amounts of carbohydrates and calories and may be interchanged.  However, if fruits or starches are regularly substituted for milk, calcium intake may be decreased.  Conversely, regularly choosing milk instead of fruits and starches may result in inadequate fiber intake.

Foods from the “other” starch list (desserts, fast food) are also interchangeable with the fruit and milk list. These items are usually higher in sugar and fat and need to be eaten within the context of a healthy meal plan.

Beans, peas, and lentils are included in the starch list.  The serving size of a 1/2 cup is counted as one starch and one very lean meat for vegetarian meal planning.  If individuals are not practicing vegetarians or use these foods more as side dishes rather than main dishes, the very lean meat exchange is not counted (1/2 cup would equal only 1 starch).

The exchange-meal plan allows for choices and flexibility.  For example, you may want to move a fruit from one meal to another, just remember to keep your meals balanced (spread your exchanges out fairly evenly throughout the day).

 

 

 

In each 2 week period, we will provide you with 2 sample meal plans (choose the meal plans closest to your daily calorie needs) to give you an example of what a typical day of eating based on our recommendations would entail.  These meal plans are to serve as a sample way to eat throughout the week, they do not need to be followed exactly day after day. Due to personal food preferences or food allergies, you may need to make alternate food choices at times.

During each 2 week block, we will also provide you with education on and recommendations for one specific area of nutrition.  Our hope is that you will use that 2 week period to focus on and improve upon this area, and then apply it on an ongoing basis when making your food choices.

We recommend keeping a daily food log to track your intake.  The log will serve as an accountability tool and an evaluation tool, should the need arise for adjustments to be made in your meal plan.
